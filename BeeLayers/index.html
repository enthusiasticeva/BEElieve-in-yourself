Antennae

Bees often live in tree cavities and hives, which have very low levels of light, so they need to be able to sense without vision. The antennae hold thousands of receptors for touch (mechanoreceptors), smell (odour receptors) and taste (gustatory receptors). Instead of pressure sensitive ear drums like humans, instead, touch receptors in their antennae sense vibrations in the air that allow them to hear airborne sound in close range.

Head

The head stores the brain, eyes and antennae. Made up of 950,000 neurones, the bee’s brain is small, but is also able to divide and perform complex tasks as well as communicate with the rest of the body.

Eye

Bees have two large compound eyes, made up of ommatidium (light focusing groups of cells). A worker bee has about about 4,000-6,000 ommatidia but drones have more 7,000-8,600, perhaps due to better vision required for mating. Like most insects, their eyes do not produce as higher quality image as humans, however they are much more sensitive to motion detection. Bees also have three smaller simple eyes on the top of their head called ocelli. These eyes don’t focus, but are very sensitive in order to inform the bee about light intensity.

Mouth

Unlike most insects, bees have mouths that can suck (like moths), and chew (like grasshoppers). To do this, they have both a jaw (mandible) and a proboscis. They use the mandible to chew wood, manipulate wax, defending against enemy bees and mites, and cleaning other bees. The proboscis is used to such honey, nectar and water, and to exchange food with other bees.

Thorax

The middles section of a bee, know as the thorax, facilitates the bees movements. It is where the bee’s two pairs of wings and three pairs of legs are connected.

Wings

Bees have two sets of wings, the front ones being larger that the hind ones. During flight, the two wings are hooked together by hooks along the edge of the hind wing and act in synchronisation. Flight is controlled by the two main muscles in the thorax, the longitudinal and vertical muscles.

Abdomen

The abdomen is the hind segment of a bee. The different types of bees in a hive (drone, worker, queen) can be differentiated by the size and shape of their abdomens. It is home to the most of the bee’s internal organs, and the stinger

Legs

Bees legs are very versatile, with claws and pads that allow them to grip on both rough and smooth surfaces, anywhere from a tree trunk to glass. On their front legs, they have a special diet used to clean pollen off their antennae. On the tibia of their back legs, bees have pollen baskets, which is used to carry pollen and propolis. It is a cupped surface with hairs along the edges and a long bristle that goes down the centre and through the pollen pellet or propolis being carried to keep it in place whilst in flight. The legs also contain special structures for pressing pollen for storage.

Stinger

The bee's stinger is a combination of internal and external organ in the abdomen. It is an evolved egg depositor - ovipositor - which has two lancets, delivering the venom made by the venom glands and stored in the venom sac. It is speculated that a bee’s stinger was inherited from their ancient wasp ancestors who would lay their eggs in the flesh of their prey. Given the stinger is a remnant of a female reproductive system, male bees generally can’t sting. Worker honey bees have barbed stingers that stick into the body of a mammal, causing part of her abdomen to be pulled out when she flies away, often killing her. Queen bees and species like bumblebees have smooth stingers, allowing them to repeatedly sting without dying.
